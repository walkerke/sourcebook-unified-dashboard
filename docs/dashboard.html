SOURCEBOOK

Understand your community's housing needs.

Sourcebook is HousingForward Virginia's online compendium of housing data. It collects, summarizes, and presents dozens of different data points to help users understand housing trends in their communities to advocate for and implement better housing solutions.

Housing and housing-related data is spread across multiple sources and can be time-consuming to clean up, analyze, and visualize. Sourcebook brings together the most important data points in one place to make that work simpler for you.

Sourcebook is an evolving and user-focused resource. We encourage you to contact us if there are particular features you would like to see, or data points that would be most helpful to your work.

Getting Started

Use the sidebar to navigate through different housing data topics:

  • Demographics: Population, age, household composition
  • Inventory: Housing stock, type, age, and characteristics
  • Economics: Income, poverty, and employment data
  • Homeownership: Sales, prices, and mortgage information
  • Rental: Rent levels, vacancy, and assisted housing
  • Affordability: Cost burden and housing gaps
  • Instability: Homelessness data
  • Economic Impact Calculators: Tools to estimate job and economic growth

Select your geographic area at the top of the sidebar, then explore the data topics that interest you most.

Housing Type

The dashboards on this page break down housing stock by the number of homes within each building. For example, a 1-unit residential building is a single-family home, while a 5-unit residential building is a small apartment complex. These data are helpful in understanding the makeup of a community's housing supply, overall density, and construction trends.

Housing type by tenure

This dashboard shows the current supply of occupied housing units among owners, renters, and all households, broken down by number of homes within each building.

All homes are classified into one of the following six categories:

  • Single-family (detached) are homes with one living unit that does not share any walls with another home.
  • Single-family (attached) are homes with one living unit that shares at least one wall with another home.
  • Small-scale multifamily are homes in buildings with 2 to 19 total units. This includes duplexes, triplexes, and other similar buildings of three stories or fewer. This typically includes low-rise and mid-rise apartments.
  • Medium-scale multifamily are homes in buildings with 20 to 49 total units. This typically includes low-rise and mid-rise apartments.
  • Large-scale multifamily are homes in buildings with 50 or more total units. This typically includes mid-rise and high-rise apartments.
  • Mobile (manufactured) home are factory-built homes that are fully built on a chassis. These homes are built to meet standards promulgated by the U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development (the "HUD Code") and are distinct from modular/prefabricated homes.

Data is available at the state, CBSA, and locality level.

NOTE: The American Community Survey also includes an 'Other' category for certain housing types. These homes include boats, recreational vehicles (RVs), vans, and all other units that do not fall into one of the above categories. We have omitted this category because the share of these 'Other' units is insignificant (less than one percent) across all geographies in Virginia.

Employment

The dashboards on this page show employment and unemployment data across Virginia. Jobs and housing are deeply intertwined—insufficient housing can hinder economic growth by limiting where workers can afford to live, while job growth without corresponding housing supply can lead to increased commute times and housing affordability challenges.

Employment levels

This dashboard shows monthly employment levels across Virginia. Employment data is presented on a non-seasonally adjusted basis, meaning it reflects actual job counts without statistical adjustments for typical seasonal patterns.

Data is available at the state, CBSA, and locality level.

Unemployment rate

This dashboard displays the percentage of the labor force that is without jobs but actively seeking work. The unemployment rate is a key economic indicator that affects housing demand and affordability in local communities.

Data is available at the state, CBSA, and locality level.

Jobs-housing growth comparison

This dashboard examines how job growth compares to housing supply growth over time. When job growth significantly outpaces housing production, communities often experience increased housing costs and longer commutes as workers are forced to live farther from employment centers.

Data is available at the state, CBSA, and locality level.

House Price Index

The dashboards on this page show the Federal Housing Finance Agency (FHFA) House Price Index, which tracks changes in single-family home prices over time. Unlike median sales prices, the HPI measures price changes for the same types of properties over time, providing a clearer picture of market appreciation trends.

The FHFA House Price Index is calculated using repeat sales and refinancing data on the same properties, which helps control for differences in the types of homes being sold. This makes it a valuable tool for understanding true price appreciation rather than changes in the mix of homes being sold.

Annual house price appreciation

This dashboard shows annual percentage changes in home prices based on the FHFA House Price Index. Positive values indicate price appreciation, while negative values indicate price declines.

Data is available at the state, CBSA, and locality level where sufficient data exists.

Cumulative price appreciation since 2000

This dashboard shows cumulative price changes since 2000, indexed to 100 in the year 2000. This long-term view helps identify major market cycles, including the housing boom and bust of the 2000s and the recovery that followed.

Data is available at the state, CBSA, and locality level where sufficient data exists.

Quarterly house price trends

This dashboard shows quarterly HPI data, providing more frequent updates on price trends than annual data. Quarterly data can help identify emerging trends and seasonal patterns in local housing markets.

Data is available at the state, CBSA, and locality level where sufficient data exists.

Federal Housing Finance Agency (FHFA) House Price Index

The FHFA HPI is a weighted, repeat-sales index measuring average price changes in repeat sales or refinancings on the same properties. The index is calculated using data from Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac repeat mortgage transactions on single-family properties.

Geographic Coverage

The HPI is available at multiple geographic levels including national, state, metropolitan statistical area (MSA), and select county/locality levels. Coverage depends on sufficient transaction volume for reliable estimates.

Home Mortgage Disclosure Act (HMDA)

The Home Mortgage Disclosure Act requires mortgage lenders to report public loan-level information about mortgage applications and originations. Data includes borrower demographics, loan characteristics, property location, and application outcomes. HMDA data is collected by the Federal Financial Institutions Examination Council (FFIEC) and made available by the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau.

Coverage and Limitations

HMDA data covers most mortgage lenders but has some limitations. Small lenders and certain types of loans may not be included. The data represents applications and originations but does not capture the full homebuying process or secondary market activity.

Gross rent is the contract rent plus the estimated monthly cost of utilities (electricity, gas, and water and sewer) and fuels (oil, coal, kerosene, wood, etc.) if these are paid by the renter (or paid for the renter by someone else).

Rental Vacancy

The dashboards on this page show rental vacancy rates across Virginia. Vacancy rates indicate the balance between rental housing supply and demand. Very low vacancy rates often signal tight rental markets that can lead to rapid rent increases, while very high vacancy rates may indicate oversupply or economic distress.

A healthy rental market typically maintains vacancy rates between 5-8 percent, providing enough turnover for household mobility while maintaining stable rents. Vacancy rates below 5 percent often indicate supply shortages, while rates above 10 percent may signal oversupply or declining demand.

Rental vacancy rate

This dashboard shows the percentage of rental housing units that are vacant and available for rent. This includes units that are currently on the rental market but does not include units that are vacant for other reasons such as renovation or seasonal use.

Data is available at the state, CBSA, and locality level.

Total vacant housing units

This dashboard shows all vacant housing units, including those for rent, for sale, sold but not yet occupied, rented but not yet occupied, and held for other reasons. This broader measure helps understand the total housing stock utilization.

Vacant units are classified into several categories:

  • For rent: Available on the rental market
  • For sale: Available for purchase
  • Sold, not occupied: Recently sold but buyer hasn't moved in
  • Rented, not occupied: Recently rented but tenant hasn't moved in
  • Seasonal/recreational: Used occasionally for recreation or seasonal occupancy
  • Other vacant: Held for other reasons including renovation

Data is available at the state, CBSA, and locality level.

Vacancy trends over time

This dashboard shows how rental vacancy rates have changed over time, helping identify market trends and cycles. Declining vacancy rates often precede periods of rent growth, while increasing vacancy rates may signal economic challenges or new supply coming to market.

Data is available at the state, CBSA, and locality level.

Assisted Rentals

The dashboards on this page show rental housing units that receive public subsidies to reduce rent costs for low- and moderate-income households. According to the National Housing Preservation Database, over 171,000 rental homes in Virginia receive subsidies to lower their rents, representing a significant portion of the affordable housing stock.

Assisted rental housing includes several major federal programs along with state and local initiatives. These programs are essential for providing housing opportunities for households that cannot afford market-rate rents.

Assisted rental units by program type

This dashboard shows assisted rental housing by program type. The Low-Income Housing Tax Credit (LIHTC) is the most common form of rental assistance in Virginia, with approximately 91,599 rental homes supported by LIHTCs provided by Virginia Housing.

Major assisted housing programs include:

  • Low-Income Housing Tax Credit (LIHTC): Properties developed with federal tax credit financing, typically affordable for 30+ years
  • Housing Choice Vouchers (Section 8): Tenant-based rental assistance that can be used in private market housing

  • Project-Based Section 8: Property-based rental assistance attached to specific housing developments
  • USDA Rural Development: Rental assistance and property development in rural areas
  • Other HUD Programs: Including Section 202 elderly housing and Section 811 disability housing

Assisted housing as share of total rental stock

This dashboard shows assisted rental housing as a percentage of the total rental housing stock. This metric helps understand how much of the rental market relies on public subsidies and the relative importance of assisted housing in different communities.

Assisted housing by affordability level

Housing Cost Burden

The dashboards on this page show housing cost burden across Virginia. Cost burden is generally defined as paying more than 30 percent of household income for housing (rent or mortgage, plus associated utilities). Severe cost burden occurs when households pay more than 50 percent of their income for housing.

Cost burden is the most widely used measure of housing affordability. Households that are cost-burdened have less money available for other necessities like food, healthcare, transportation, and education. In Virginia, nearly half a million renter households (45 percent) are cost burdened.

Cost burden by income level

This dashboard shows cost burden rates by Area Median Income (AMI) categories. Most of the cost-burdened renter households in Virginia are those earning 50 percent AMI or below. Extremely low-income (ELI) households earning 30% AMI or less face particularly severe challenges, with 85 percent being cost-burdened and 65 percent being severely cost-burdened.

Income categories include:

  • Extremely Low Income (ELI): 30% AMI and below
  • Very Low Income (VLI): 31-50% AMI
  • Low Income: 51-80% AMI
  • Moderate Income: 81-100% AMI
  • Above Moderate Income: Over 100% AMI

Data is available at the state, CBSA, and locality level.

Cost burden by race and ethnicity

This dashboard shows cost burden rates by race and ethnicity. Due to historical and ongoing disparities in income and wealth, households of color often experience higher rates of cost burden than white households.

Data is available at the state, CBSA, and locality level.

Cost burden by tenure (owners vs. renters)

This dashboard compares cost burden rates between homeowners and renters. Renters typically experience higher cost burden rates than owners, particularly at lower income levels. This reflects both the lower incomes of renter households and the lack of rent-restricted affordable housing.

Data is available at the state, CBSA, and locality level.

Rental Housing Gap

The dashboards on this page show the rental housing gap, which measures the mismatch between the supply of affordable rental housing and the demand from low-income households. In Virginia, there are over 300,000 cost-burdened renters who have very low incomes, resulting in a corresponding gap of over 300,000 deeply affordable rental units.

The rental housing gap analysis compares the number of renter households at different income levels to the supply of rental units that are affordable and available to them. A gap occurs when there are more households than affordable units at a given income level.

Affordable and available units by income level

This dashboard shows the supply of rental housing that is both affordable and available to households at different income levels. A unit is considered 'affordable' if it costs no more than 30% of a household's income. A unit is 'available' if it is either vacant or occupied by a household in the target income range.

Income levels are expressed as percentages of Area Median Income (AMI):

  • Extremely Low Income: 30% AMI and below (approximately $24,000 for a family of four in Virginia)
  • Very Low Income: 31-50% AMI (approximately $24,001-$40,000 for a family of four)
  • Low Income: 51-80% AMI (approximately $40,001-$64,000 for a family of four)

Data is available at the state, CBSA, and locality level.

Housing gap by income level

This dashboard shows the shortage or surplus of affordable rental housing at each income level. Negative numbers indicate a shortage (more households than affordable units), while positive numbers indicate a surplus (more affordable units than households).

The analysis reveals that Virginia has significant shortages of housing affordable to extremely low-income and very low-income renters, while having adequate or surplus supply for moderate and higher-income renters.

Data is available at the state, CBSA, and locality level.

Gap trends over time

This dashboard shows how the rental housing gap has changed over time, helping identify whether affordability challenges are improving or worsening. Increasing gaps indicate growing affordability challenges, while decreasing gaps suggest improving conditions.

Data is available at the state, CBSA, and locality level.

Methodology Notes

The analysis assumes that higher-income households occupy lower-cost units when available, which reduces availability for lower-income households. HUD AMI Limits

The dashboards on this page show Area Median Income (AMI) limits published annually by the U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development. These income limits are used by most federal, state, and local affordable housing programs to determine household eligibility and set rent and purchase price limits.

AMI is calculated for each metropolitan area and non-metropolitan county based on median family income data from the American Community Survey. HUD adjusts these figures for high housing cost areas and publishes income limits at various percentages of AMI for different household sizes.

Current AMI limits by household size

This dashboard shows current AMI limits for different household sizes in Virginia. Income limits are provided for household sizes from 1 to 8 persons, with standard categories including:

  • Extremely Low Income: 30% AMI
  • Very Low Income: 50% AMI
  • Low Income: 80% AMI
  • Moderate Income: 100% AMI (Area Median Income)
  • Above Moderate Income: 120% AMI

Data is available at the CBSA and non-metropolitan county level.

AMI trends over time

This dashboard shows how AMI limits have changed over time, helping track income trends and understand the changing thresholds for affordable housing program eligibility. AMI limits typically increase annually but may fluctuate based on economic conditions.

Data is available at the CBSA and non-metropolitan county level.

Rent and purchase price limits

This dashboard shows maximum rent and home purchase prices corresponding to different AMI levels. These limits are calculated assuming households pay no more than 30% of income for housing costs and are used to set affordability requirements in housing programs.

For rental housing:

  • Rent limits include utilities
  • Based on 30% of gross household income
  • Used for Low-Income Housing Tax Credit, Housing Choice Voucher, and other rental programs

For homeownership:

  • Purchase price limits based on mortgage capacity
  • Include principal, interest, taxes, insurance, and utilities (PITIU)
  • Used for first-time homebuyer and affordable homeownership programs

Data is available at the CBSA and non-metropolitan county level.

HUD Area Median Income (AMI) and Income Limits

HUD publishes income limits annually for all metropolitan areas and non-metropolitan counties. These limits are based on median family income data from the American Community Survey, with adjustments for high housing cost areas and other factors as specified in federal regulations.

Calculation Methodology

Income limits are calculated as percentages of Area Median Income, adjusted for family size. HUD may limit year-over-year changes and apply minimum income limits in some areas. The limits are updated annually and typically released each spring for the program year beginning October 1.

Geographic Coverage

Income limits are published for all HUD Metro Fair Market Rent Areas (HMFAs) and non-metropolitan counties. In Virginia, this includes all metropolitan statistical areas and individual rural counties not part of metropolitan areas.

Homelessness

The dashboards on this page show homelessness data for Virginia's Continuum of Care (CoC) regions. This data comes from annual Point-in-Time (PIT) counts that HUD requires all CoCs to conduct to count people experiencing homelessness in their communities.

Virginia has 16 Continuums of Care, including a Virginia Balance of State CoC that covers rural areas not served by other CoCs. CoC regions include major metropolitan areas like Greater Richmond, Southeastern Virginia (Norfolk/Virginia Beach area), and Arlington County, as well as smaller communities throughout the state.

Point-in-Time homelessness counts

This dashboard shows the annual Point-in-Time counts of people experiencing homelessness. PIT counts include people living in emergency shelters, transitional housing programs, and Safe Havens. Counts of unsheltered individuals (those living in places not meant for human habitation) occur every other year in odd-numbered years.

HUD defines homelessness to include four categories:

  • People living in places not meant for human habitation, emergency shelters, or Safe Havens
  • People who will lose their primary residence within 14 days and lack resources to remain housed
  • Families with children or unaccompanied youth who are unstably housed and likely to continue in that state
  • People fleeing or attempting to flee domestic violence who lack alternative housing and resources

Data is available by Continuum of Care region.

Sheltered vs. unsheltered homelessness

This dashboard shows the breakdown between sheltered and unsheltered homelessness. Sheltered homelessness includes people staying in emergency shelters, transitional housing, and Safe Havens. Unsheltered homelessness includes people living in places not meant for human habitation such as cars, abandoned buildings, or outdoors.

The balance between sheltered and unsheltered homelessness varies significantly by region and can reflect differences in shelter capacity, climate, local policies, and service availability.

Data is available by Continuum of Care region.

Homelessness by population type

This dashboard shows homelessness by population subgroups, including families with children, unaccompanied youth, veterans, and chronically homeless individuals. Different populations may have different service needs and pathways out of homelessness.

Key population categories include:

  • Families with children: Households with at least one adult and one child under age 18
  • Unaccompanied youth: Persons under age 25 who are not part of a family with children
  • Veterans: Persons who served in the U.S. military
  • Chronically homeless: Individuals with disabilities who have been continuously homeless for a year or more, or have experienced at least four episodes of homelessness in the past three years

Data is available by Continuum of Care region.

Homelessness trends over time

This dashboard shows how homelessness has changed over time in Virginia's CoC regions. Trend data helps identify whether homelessness is increasing, decreasing, or remaining stable, and can inform resource allocation and policy decisions.

Data is available by Continuum of Care region from 2007 to present.
